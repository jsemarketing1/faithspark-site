I tried to read the Bible consistently for years before it actually stuck. I'd start strong, lose the thread somewhere in Leviticus, and give up until New Year's when I'd try again. The full chapter-a-day approach worked for about two weeks, then life happened and I fell behind and I'd never catch back up.

What finally worked was something much smaller. One verse. Every morning. Before I looked at my phone.

That's it. Not a chapter. Not a reading plan. One verse — with an explanation that told me what it meant and why it mattered — every single morning without fail. Two years into that habit, my faith life is completely different from what it was. Not because of discipline. Because of compounding.

## Why Small Is the Right Starting Point

There's a reason "read your Bible more" is one of the most-broken resolutions in Christian life. It's too vague, too big, and too easy to fall behind on. The moment you miss three days, guilt kicks in and you feel like you're starting over.

A daily Bible verse habit is different because it's small enough that there's no catching up required. You don't miss a verse on Tuesday and owe two on Wednesday. Each day is its own. That's the whole point.

The power isn't in how much you read in any single session. It's in the cumulative effect of showing up every day for months and years. Two minutes a day for two years is 24 hours of Scripture time you wouldn't have had otherwise.

## The Three Elements of a Habit That Sticks

Habit research is pretty clear on this: every successful habit has a trigger, a routine, and a reward.

**Trigger:** The same moment every day that cues the behavior. For my verse habit, the trigger is making my first cup of coffee. Before I sit down, before I check anything, I open FaithSpark. The coffee is the trigger. If you're a morning person, your trigger might be waking up. If you do better at night, it might be when you plug your phone in.

**Routine:** The actual behavior — simple, specific, repeatable. Open the [daily Bible verse app](/daily-bible-verse-app), read the verse, read the explanation, sit with it for 60 seconds. That's the routine. Two minutes, maybe three. Nothing more complicated than that.

**Reward:** The feeling you get after. This one's personal, but for me it's a kind of grounding — a sense that my day is starting from the right place. That's the reward that keeps me coming back. Over time, the habit also builds reward in itself: you notice you know more Scripture, you notice it showing up in your thinking when you need it, you notice yourself feeling more anchored on hard days.

## The Long Game

The daily Bible verse habit isn't dramatic. It's quiet. Some days a verse really lands and I think about it for hours. Some days it's just words and I move on. Over time, the words accumulate. Scripture starts to show up in your thinking at unexpected moments — in a conversation, in a hard decision, when you're sitting with someone in grief and the right thing to say comes from somewhere deeper than you expected.

That's the long game. Not becoming a Bible scholar. Becoming someone who carries the Word with them because it's been part of every day for long enough that it's just part of you.
