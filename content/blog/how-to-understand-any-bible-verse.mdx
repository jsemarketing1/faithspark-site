I grew up going to church but I spent most of my life pretending I understood the Bible more than I did. Someone would quote a verse in a sermon and I'd nod like it made sense, then go home and look it up and still feel lost. The context was missing. I knew the words but not the story behind them.

That changed when I stopped reading verses in isolation and started reading them like what they actually are: letters and poems and histories and prophecy written by real people in real situations, to real audiences with real problems.

Once you understand *who* wrote something and *why*, the words start making sense. You don't need a seminary degree. You just need a few basic moves.

## The Single Most Important Move: Read What Comes Before and After

This sounds obvious but almost nobody does it. If you look up a single verse, you're missing the sentence it belongs to — which is often missing the paragraph it belongs to — which is missing the chapter and the whole book.

Philippians 4:13 — "I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me" — is one of the most quoted verses in the Bible. It gets printed on gym bags and coffee mugs. But most people don't know it was written by Paul from prison. In context, the verse is about contentment in any circumstance, including suffering — not about athletic achievement.

Context changes everything.

## Ask These Three Questions Before You Read

Before you dig into any verse, ask:

**1. Who wrote this?** Was it a king (David in the Psalms)? A prophet in exile (Isaiah, Jeremiah)? An apostle in prison (Paul in Philippians)? A fisherman writing to early believers (Peter)? Knowing who wrote it tells you their emotional state, their circumstances, and what they were trying to say.

**2. Who were they writing to?** The Psalms are personal prayers and songs. The letters (Romans, Corinthians, Galatians) were written to specific churches dealing with specific problems. Revelation was written to persecuted Christians in the first century. Understanding the audience changes how you read the message.

**3. What's the big theme of this book?** Each book of the Bible has a through-line. Genesis is about origins and covenant. Exodus is about deliverance. John is about belief. Romans is about the gospel explained carefully. When you know the theme, the individual passages fit into a larger picture.

## Hard Passages: What to Do When You're Stuck

Some verses genuinely are hard. The book of Job wrestles with suffering in a way that doesn't have easy answers. Some of Revelation is highly symbolic and scholars disagree on its interpretation. Paul's letters sometimes assume knowledge of Jewish law that a modern reader doesn't have.

When you hit a hard passage, here's what works:

- **Read more context.** The answer is almost always in the surrounding chapters.
- **Ask what the verse is NOT saying.** Misunderstanding often comes from reading something into a verse that isn't there.
- **Sit with the question.** Not every hard verse resolves quickly. Some are meant to be prayed over and returned to.
- **Use a resource like FaithSpark** that gives you the background without overwhelming you with technical commentary.

The goal isn't to master every difficult passage before you can benefit from the Bible. It's to keep reading, keep asking questions, and let God meet you in the parts you do understand while you grow into the parts you don't.
