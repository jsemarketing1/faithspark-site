I'm a truck driver. My alarm goes off before most people have had their first thought of the day. I used to grab my phone and start checking things — news, weather, routes, messages. By the time I was actually out the door I'd already consumed enough noise to make my head loud for hours.

A morning prayer routine changed that. Not because I became a morning person. Not because I suddenly had extra time. But because the first five minutes of my day started shaping the rest of them in a way that nothing else did.

Here's what I actually did — and what I'd tell someone starting from scratch.

## Why Morning Is the Right Time for Most People

Your mind in the morning is quieter than at any other point in the day. Before the emails, the texts, the news, the tasks — before any of that gets in — there's a window. It's short. For some people it's five minutes, for others it's thirty. But it exists, and what you put into it sets the tone for everything that follows.

That's why a morning prayer routine works when a prayer-at-some-point-today habit doesn't. The morning window is protected in a way that the middle of the afternoon isn't. When you claim it for prayer before you claim it for anything else, you actually end up praying.

The science backs this up too. Decision fatigue means that willpower is stronger earlier in the day. The habits you want to build are more likely to stick if you do them in the morning before your mental resources get used up on everything else.

## The Morning Prayer Routine I Actually Use

I've kept this simple because simple is what I can maintain on 5 hours of sleep and a 6am departure.

**Step 1: No phone first.** The phone goes on the nightstand when I wake up, not in my hand. This is the hardest part and the most important part. Even two minutes of Instagram before prayer changes the mental state you bring to it.

**Step 2: One minute of stillness.** Before I say anything to God, I try to be quiet for about a minute. This sounds easy. It isn't. But it's the thing that makes the prayer feel like a conversation instead of a monologue.

**Step 3: Open FaithSpark.** The morning prayer session in FaithSpark is about five minutes. Spark leads me through gratitude, surrender, requests, and a closing verse. I follow along. Some mornings I add to it. Some mornings I just receive what's there.

**Step 4: Read today's verse.** After the prayer, I read the daily verse and the explanation. This is what I carry with me into the day. I try to think about it at least once more before noon.

That's the whole routine. Twelve minutes on a slow morning. Seven on a fast one.

## Starting When You've Never Done It Before

If you've never had a morning prayer routine, start smaller than you think you should.

One minute. Literally. Wake up, before you grab your phone, say one sentence to God. Thank Him for something. Ask for help with something. One sentence.

Do that for a week without missing a day. Then add the FaithSpark guided prayer session. Then add the daily verse.

The reason people fail at morning prayer isn't that they don't want to pray. It's that they try to start a 30-minute routine overnight and then feel like failures when they miss day two.

Small is sustainable. Sustainable becomes real.

## What to Do on the Hard Mornings

Some mornings you won't want to pray. You'll be tired, resentful, too busy, too numb. Those mornings matter more than the easy ones.

Here's what I do on those mornings: I open FaithSpark anyway and let Spark lead. I don't try to feel it. I just show up. Sometimes by the end of five minutes something has shifted. Sometimes it hasn't. But I showed up, and that matters more than the feeling.

The mornings when prayer feels most mechanical are often the ones that plant something in you that you don't notice until later — a patience you didn't have in a hard conversation, a peace that arrives out of nowhere at the right moment, a verse that comes back to you at 2pm when you need it.

Prayer doesn't always feel like it's working when you're doing it. It shows up in your life in ways that are harder to trace.
